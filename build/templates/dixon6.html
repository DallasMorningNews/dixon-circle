The kid who got out

In Dixon today, hope and desolation live on opposite sides of the same street.

NBA All Star forward Larry Johnson gave $1 million to the city of Dallas to build this recreation center in the neighborhood where he grew up. (File photo)

As a boy, Larry Johnson liked to fight. As punishment, he was sent to boxing classes with the cops.

Boxing led to basketball, and basketball led Larry out of Dixon. The Charlotte Hornets took him in the 1990s, then the New York Knicks.

If Dixon Circle has a legend, a hero—it is Larry Johnson. Not only did he become an NBA star, but for a long time he kept coming back to the neighborhood he called home, raising Dixon up with him.

A structure remains un-built along Dixon Avenue in the Dixon Circle neighborhood of Dallas Monday August 1, 2016. It has been more than four years since the police-involved shooting and death of James Harper on July 24, 2012. (Andy Jacobsohn/Staff Photographer)

Johnson gave $1 million to the city to build a recreation center that is now the neighborhood’s sanctuary. No drugs here, no bullets. The sound of children’s laughter and splashing fountains mask the traffic and disorder across the street like a force field.

Across the avenue from the park, a lattice of steel stands two stories high in a patch of weeds. It has stood like that for ten years. And this was also Johnson’s hand.

Some in the neighborhood say it was going to be a rehab center, or maybe a church. He had intended to build a child care facility, according to city records. No one involved with the project returned calls about it.

Johnson lives in New York now, and isn’t seen in Dixon anymore.
