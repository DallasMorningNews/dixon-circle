The kid who got out

In Dixon, hope and desolation live on opposite sides of the same street. Former NBA star and Dixon resident Larry Johnson found success despite his surroundings. Not everyone is so fortunate.

NBA All Star forward Larry Johnson gave $1 million to the city of Dallas to build this recreation center in the neighborhood where he grew up. (File photo)

The boy liked to get in fights. He was sent to boxing classes with the cops as punishment.

It worked.

Larry Johnson became an NBA star in the ‘90s, first with the Charlotte Hornets, then with the New York Knicks.

One night he cruised into Dixon in his Benz and parked by the old basketball court. A patrol car pulled up.

“This is a drug-infested neighborhood,” an officer told Johnson. “What are you doing here?"

He told the officer it was home.

Johnson donated $1 million to the city of Dallas in 1995 so it could build a recreation center on Dixon Avenue. It became the neighborhood’s sanctuary.

A structure remains un-built along Dixon Avenue in the Dixon Circle neighborhood of Dallas Monday August 1, 2016. It has been more than four years since the police-involved shooting and death of James Harper on July 24, 2012. (Andy Jacobsohn/Staff Photographer)

There are no drugs here, no bullets. Only children’s laughter and families splayed on the grass, soaking up the sun, as if protected by a force field.

Across the street is the metal frame of a building, forgotten in a patch of weeds. That was also Johnson’s hand.

It was supposed to be a child care facility, according to city records. Construction began in 2006 and never finished.

It’s unclear why Johnson and his associates abandoned the plans. He lives in New York now, where he shows off Lexus cars for charity.

He doesn’t come to Dixon anymore.

Mike Mike – the man unceremoniously shot in the leg outside Dixon’s corner store in April – also could have made the pros, according to some residents. They say he would have at least gotten a scholarship, had he finished high school.

Now his neighbors watch him limp along on crutches to pick up his daughter from summer camp at the rec.

He doesn’t play basketball anymore.
